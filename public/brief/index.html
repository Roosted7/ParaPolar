ALT 3000 m the thesis

Nobody falls in love with a graph. They fall in love with what it lets them feel.

Every paragliding student meets the polar curve the same way: a static figure in a theory book, memorized for the exam, forgotten at the landing field. Yet the ideas behind it — speed to fly, wind gradient, MacCready — are the difference between gliding home and landing in a tree.

ParaPolar's edge is that it can close the gap between the figure and the feeling. The redesign has one organizing rule: the consequence view is the hero; the chart is the instrument. You watch a wing fly over terrain, you push the bar, you feel the trade — and the polar graph explains what you just felt, not the other way around.

"A flight simulator for understanding, not for controls."

ALT 2000 m reach — the loop, not the megaphone

Growth is a ridge lift: set up the cycle and stay in it

The current site is a JavaScript app with almost no indexable text — to a search engine, parapolar.com is nearly a blank page with a good title. Fixing that is the single highest-leverage SEO move, and it doubles as real user value:

Learn pages

Pre-rendered articles in all four languages — "What is a polar curve?", "Speed to fly, explained", "MacCready without tears" — each with the live widget embedded mid-article. Static HTML for crawlers, playground for humans.

Embeds for schools

A one-line iframe embed of any scenario. Flight schools and blogs get a free teaching widget; ParaPolar gets the backlinks that actually move rankings in a niche this small.

Share cards

Permalinks already encode full state. Render them as OG images (worker-generated) so a shared "look, negative groundspeed!" link unfurls as a picture in WhatsApp/Telegram — where pilot communities actually live.

Challenges

"Cross this valley losing the least height." Score, retry, send to your club group. Each challenge link is a lesson wearing a game's clothes — and another inbound link.

Distribution to seed the loop: instructor outreach (schools in DE/FR/NL/AT/CH first — the domains already speak their languages), r/freeflight and paraglidingforum threads that show rather than tell, and one collaboration with a paragliding YouTube channel walking through backwind flight on the tool.

And one subtraction: replace Google Analytics with a cookieless option (Cloudflare Web Analytics or Plausible). The cookie banner is the first thing every new visitor sees today — the worst possible first impression for a toy. Delete the banner, gain three seconds of magic.

ALT 1500 m the UX spine

One scene, one loop, layers of depth

Scene first
The ground view fills the screen; the polar graph docks as a translucent inspector (desktop) or a pull-up sheet (mobile). Both are views of the same moment — hover the polar, the wing responds; watch the wing, the dot rides the curve.
Direct manipulation
Kill the slider farm. Drag the wing itself forward/back to change speed. Drag the windsock to set wind. Drag the thermal to set lift. The controls live in the world they affect — sliders remain as a precision fallback in the inspector.
Ten-second aha
On first load: wing flying at trim, a gentle prompt — "a headwind is coming" — wind rises, groundspeed melts, the ghost of the best-glide tangent appears. One scripted beat, then it hands you the controls. No modal, no tour, no banner.
Progressive depth
Simple mode = speed + wind, nothing else. Every advanced concept unlocks in context: the first time your groundspeed goes negative, a card offers the "Backwind" lesson. Curiosity pulls the curriculum, not a settings toggle.
Felt consequences
Stall isn't a number turning red: the wing pitches back, shudders, drops — the screen dips with it (respecting reduced-motion). Speed-to-fly isn't a row in a table: it's a notch on the speed control you can feel the knob snap to.
Numbers with dignity
Readouts styled as flight instruments — tabular numerals, unit-aware (m/s, ft/min, kt), glanceable. Data is never decoration; every visible number is one a pilot would actually check.

Less dashboard, more cockpit at golden hour

The current UI is competent Tailwind vernacular: white cards, slate borders, rounded pills. Nothing wrong — nothing memorable. The replacement borrows from the two most beautiful things pilots already stare at: the evening sky and their instruments.

Roadmap: three glides

Now — the feel

weeks, not months
  • Kill the cookie banner (cookieless analytics)
  • Scene-first layout: ground view hero, polar as inspector, linked highlighting between them
  • Direct manipulation v1: drag wing = speed, drag windsock = wind, STF notch on the control
  • Design language pass: dusk palette, instrument pods, type system

Next — the pull

the growth loop
  • Learn pages ×4 languages, pre-rendered, widget embedded (this is the SEO play)
  • OG share cards from permalinks (worker-rendered)
  • Embed mode + outreach kit for flight schools
  • Ten-second scripted opening beat

Later — the game

retention & love
  • Lesson mode: 8–10 scenes from "what is a polar" to MacCready, exam-aligned per country
  • Challenges with scores and share links ("cross the valley", "escape the venturi")
  • Glider comparison overlay · classroom mode
  • Sound design: wind volume = airspeed (vario already sings)

How we'll know it works: median session > 3 minutes; ≥ 30% of sessions touch a lesson or challenge; shared-link opens per week; embeds live on ≥ 10 school sites; "polar curve paragliding" top-3 in DE/FR/NL. All measurable without a single cookie.
